How to Plan a Roof Replacement When You Have Solar Panels in Portland

May 2026 6 min read Portland Metro

Most Portland homeowners with solar panels don't realize the roof replacement and solar removal need to be sequenced — not just scheduled on the same week. Get the order wrong and you're looking at voided warranties, delays, and a roofer who won't start until the panels are gone.

This is one of the most common mistakes we see across the Portland metro. A homeowner gets a roofing quote, the roofer says "just have someone take the panels down before we start," and the homeowner assumes they can figure it out a few days before. Then they find out solar removal requires a licensed electrician, a permit from the county, and 2–4 weeks of lead time — right in the middle of Portland's 8-week roofing season.

Here's how to plan it correctly so both crews are properly sequenced and your system is back online as fast as possible.

Step 1: Get Your Solar Removal Quote Before You Book a Roofer

This is the single most important thing to get right. Your solar removal crew needs to be on the job before your roofer starts — and getting a removal quote takes time, permits take time, and scheduling takes time.

In Clackamas, Washington, and Multnomah counties, solar removal permits typically take one to four weeks to process. If you book your roofer first and then start looking for a solar crew, you will almost certainly cause a scheduling conflict that delays your entire project.

The right sequence: get your solar removal quote first, confirm availability, then book your roofer around the solar crew's schedule — not the other way around.

Portland Roofing Season Reality

The reliable dry window for roofing across the Portland metro runs roughly mid-July through mid-September — about 8 weeks. Roofing contractors fill that window fast. If you're planning a summer roof replacement, start the solar removal process no later than May or June.

Step 2: Verify Your Solar Crew Is Properly Licensed in Oregon

Oregon requires solar removal and reinstallation to be performed by a contractor holding both a valid Construction Contractors Board (CCB) license and an Oregon electrical license. This isn't optional — and it matters for your warranty.

Most panel manufacturers and inverter manufacturers require licensed electrical work for their warranties to remain valid. If an unlicensed crew removes and reinstalls your system and something fails later, you may have no warranty coverage.

Before you hire anyone, ask two questions directly: "What is your Oregon CCB number?" and "Can you show me your electrical license?" Any legitimate contractor will answer without hesitation.

Step 3: Understand What Happens With Your Utility Connection

When your solar panels come down, your system is de-energized — which technically affects your interconnection agreement with your utility, whether that's PGE or Pacific Power depending on where you are in the metro.

The good news: a temporary removal for roof work does not require you to reapply for net metering. Your agreement stays in place. However, your utility does need to be notified before the system is de-energized. A reputable solar removal contractor handles this notification as part of the job — if they don't mention it, ask about it explicitly.

Your net metering billing will be paused during the downtime and resume automatically once the system is back online and producing.

Step 4: Coordinate the Handoff Between Solar Crew and Roofer

The cleanest jobs happen when the solar removal crew and the roofing contractor communicate directly — not through you as the go-between. Ask both crews if they're willing to exchange contact information and confirm the handoff date together.

What your roofer needs from the solar crew: confirmation that all panels, racking, and flashing penetrations have been removed and that the deck is ready for new underlayment. What the solar crew needs from your roofer: confirmation that the new roof is fully complete and cured before reinstallation begins.

  1. Solar removal crew removes all panels, racking, and hardware

    Panels are labeled, documented, and stored securely on your property.

  2. Roofing crew completes full tear-off and new roof installation

    They work on a clean deck with no hardware in the way.

  3. Solar crew returns for reinstallation

    New penetrations are sealed to manufacturer specs. System is reconnected and tested.

  4. Post-reinstall performance test confirms output

    You get a written report confirming your system is producing at pre-removal levels.

How Long Will Your System Be Down?

For a typical Portland metro home with a 15–25 panel system, the removal itself takes half a day to a full day. Reinstallation takes roughly the same. The time in between depends entirely on how long your roofer needs — most standard residential re-roofs in the metro take 1–3 days.

Total system downtime from removal to reinstallation is typically 3 to 7 days for a well-coordinated project. Poorly coordinated projects — where there are scheduling gaps between crews — can stretch to two to three weeks.
